Why these weights?

JDII DASHBOARD / RECOMMENDATIONS

Key Insights & Recommendations

Translating inflation findings into actionable decisions for employers, candidates, and HR teams

6 Key Findings from This Analysis
THE NUMBERS THAT TELL THE INFLATION STORY
01
JDII score increased 29.2% between 2018 and 2025 (67.2 → 86.8), confirmed by linear regression with R²=0.83, p<0.05.
02
Average experience requirements grew from 5.13 → 7.09 years — a near-perfect linear trend (R²=0.998) with no slowdown in sight.
03
94% of postings demand a post-graduate qualification (MBA 45%, Master's 28%, PhD 20%) — only 6% accept a Bachelor's degree.
04
Remote jobs have statistically significantly higher JDII scores than onsite jobs (82.66 vs 78.37, t=9.78, p<0.001).
05
Python demand rose from 41.8% → 54.6% of postings between 2018 and 2025 — the fastest-growing skill in the dataset.
06
At current trend, the average JDII score will reach ~99.8 by 2028 — effectively maxing out the scale, implying a saturation point is approaching.

🏢 For Employers & HR Teams

  • Audit job descriptions against actual role requirements — separate must-have skills from nice-to-have to reduce JDII score.
  • Reconsider blanket MBA/Master's requirements for roles where a Bachelor's and strong domain experience is sufficient.
  • Cap skills listed at 6–8 genuinely required skills — longer lists reduce candidate quality without improving hiring outcomes.
  • Eliminate corporate buzzwords ("10x engineer", "go-getter") — they signal poor JD quality and deter strong candidates.
  • Benchmark JDs against JDII scores to identify which roles are over-specified before posting.

👩‍💼 For Job Seekers & Candidates

  • Don't self-reject — JDII analysis shows that most JDs are inflated beyond actual minimum requirements. Apply if you meet 60–70% of listed criteria.
  • Prioritize Python, SQL, and Power BI — these appear in the highest share of postings across all industries.
  • Target entry-level to mid-level postings (JDII 51–66) for more realistic requirements before moving to senior roles.
  • Focus on roles where salary is commensurate with the JDII score — Technology and Finance offer the highest compensation.
  • Understand that MBA requirements in 45% of postings reflects credential inflation — real screening criteria may be more flexible.

📊 For Researchers & Policy Makers

  • The JDII trajectory (approaching 100 by 2028) suggests a natural ceiling — monitor whether requirements begin to moderate post-saturation.
  • Intervention may be needed: industry self-regulation or standardized JD frameworks could slow credential inflation.
  • Remote job inflation warrants investigation — remote roles should require less location-specific experience, yet show higher JDII scores.
  • Education policy should account for MBA over-indexing: if 45% of roles demand an MBA, access and affordability become equity issues.

🔭 Future Research Directions

  • Extend the dataset post-2025 to test whether the JDII saturation ceiling hypothesis holds true.
  • Add a "matched hiring" column — track whether high-JDII postings actually result in successful hires to measure real impact.
  • Build a real-time JDII scorer that accepts any job description text as input and returns an inflation score.
  • Expand geographic analysis — compare JDII patterns across cities and countries to identify regional inflation differences.
  • Train a classification model to predict JDII category (Low/Moderate/High/Extreme) from raw JD text using NLP.
